Section 1: Basics (Phonology and Writing System)

This section will cover the absolute basics of the writing system and phonology, enough to be able to read and have an idea how to pronounce Taisroan.

Alphabet

Taisroan has a few various types of scripts, but for the purpose of initially exhibiting its phonetics, it will be displayed in its Standard Alphabet. The alphabet form is, as you would expect, an alphabet. It is written left to right horizontally, with spaces between words, and punctuation usually separating sentences or clauses. Taisroans have other writing systems, such as a syllabic block form, an abjad, and a cusrive varient of this main script. We can get to those later. It is notable, though, that all of the writing systems come with a 1:1 phoneme-grapheme ratio, so spelling shouldn't be too hard. The alphabet is split up into families, which is worth noting.

Vowels are the first family, and they come in two variants, sharp ¿¿¿¿¿¿ or Major letters ¿¿¿¿¿ and dull ¿¿¿¿¿ or minor letters ¿¿¿¿¿.

Then the first group of consonants is the S Family. This group is is a bit different from the rest of the consonants. They are drawn with voiced sounds sharp. This is an exception, to the norm, but these are normally listed first in the Taisroan letter order.

The first pair sounds like the usual English "s" and "z". The second pair sounds close to "sh" as in "show", and the "s" in "pleasure", and the third pair are similar to "ch" in "choice" and "j" in "join". The sounds are slightly closer to those found in Japanese, however.

For the rest of the consonants, there's some consistent patterns where voiced sounds are the dull ones, and are drawn with curves in similar shapes to their angular counterparts, which are unvoiced sounds. The one odd case is the relation between r and l, the Taisroan "r" sound is tapped and not really velarized at all, similar, again, to a Japanese r, or the tongue flap when pronouncing "little" fast in American English.

The color coding is how they group the letters, and how it works phonetically. The shades of blue are tongue sounds, the purples are nasals, the oranges approximates, and the last h is on its own because it's lonely and green and that's just not a creative color. These groups will become important later on when discussing how these letters are organized into syllables.

Stops are placed with a space before and after. They mark clause separation, similar to a comma.

Fullstops, I-marks and E-marks are placed directly after a word, then a space afterward. These operate quite the same as English punctuation, fullstops, question marks, and exclamation marks respectively.

Quotes are used for names and quoted dialogue. Punctuation in quoted dialogue goes within quotes. If quotes are used to isolate words, punctuation goes outside the quotes. Stops are not needed for quote braces, as they are built into them.

Corner Braces may be used for emphasis or vague quoting. Brackets are used for some things, mostly in the numerical system.

Double stops tend to come before a clarification statement, related clause, or a list, like an English colon or semicolon.

The number system throughout Taisroa has Base-10 numerals, which means just 10 digits. Though later we'll see their culture operates on Base-8 things. Seeing as Taisroans have 10 fingers, counting with 10 had won out as logical over time.

Places have specific names as well. As seen in the table on the right, ten, hundred, and thousand have unique names. At ten thousand, the system starts to change. Taisroan groups large numbers into groups of four zeroes. Notice the initial consonants for the names of the numbers on the left table. These also become the initial consonants for the place value of every 4 digits added. For instance,1,0000 is suq, "suq"; like sifon, "sifon". It has 1 group of four zeroes. So, lets take a number with 4 groups of zeroes; 1,0000,0000,0000,0000 would be śuq, "śuq", like śim "śim". The Taisroans have not yet needed a number above the vuq range. For reference, one vuq would be 1,0000,0000,0000,0000,0000,0000,0000,0000,0000,0000. English does not even have a word for this number.

The system also uses a Place-Value System, and numbers are said quite literally. The format goes [number-place, number-place] etc. So, 27 would be said "þaf-dek nem"; 724 would be said "nem-pék þaf-dek śim". Once you get above the ten-thousand range, you group them in thousands. 63,2413 would be said "kás-dek cén-suq, þaf-źek śim-pék sifon-dek cén.", Which has a more distinct rhythm than "six hundred thirty two thousand four hundred thirteen", and just about as many syllables.

The numbers are written in one of two ways. The first is a place-value method, akin to our Arabic numerals. 21,625 would be written 2,1625. Taisroan seperates groups of four zeroes with stops like we commonly seperate groups of 3 zeroes with commas. the system is simple enough.

Another system, most commonly used for abreviating longer numbers, is Taisroan Notation. It works by using the digit, and then the initial of the place value corresponding to it. The full value is placed in brackets. For instance, 20 would be [2d]. 4,0013 would be [4s,1d,3]. This may seem a bit convoluted, but useful if a number is an estimate or rounded large number, such as 7,0000,0000,0000, or if it has a long gap, such as 41,0000,0013. These can be condenced as [7c] and [4d1þ,1d,3] respectively.

With this system, it's also common to mix cases, eliminating some number markers and only designating "suq" groups. For instance, the above number could also be written [41þ,13].

Section 2: Word Building

This section goes over the next step up in competency, syllables and word building. This will cover how exactly taisroan phonetics work in combination.

Phonetic Rules & Constraints

Word Structure in Taisroan is similar to Japanese. Syllables in general have a CV(N) construction, meaning there is usually a consonant and a vowel, and sometimes a nasal consonant before the next consonant, and sometimes don't have an initial consonant. There is one more rule that is different from Japanese phonetic constraints, and that is the addition of Final Consonants. At the end of a word, but not the end of any syllable, the optional nasal could also be an optional final, which include all of the Sharp Consonants, with the addition of l. To better organize what I just said, I put it in a table.

Nasals do not come before final consonants, if a word ends in a nasal consonant, it cannot end in a final consonant. Combonations like ent cannot happen.

Now that the ground rules are laid down, there are also some exceptions.

First off, Because of the compounding nature of words, it is common for two voiced consonants to be next to each other only if they are the end and beginning of two joined words. A prime example of this exception is the word taisroa, "taisroa" for "Eight-world".

Single vowel syllables are not that common and very very rarely come adjacent to each other. Adjacent vowels in general, however, can and do occur.

Another important theme throughout Taisroan is the concept of sized sounds and sound inversion.

ra ra big/major
si si small/minor
zí zí none

Sized Sounds have to do with open and lower vowels sounding "bigger", and higher placed vowels sounding "smaller". Taisroans use much contrast of i and a vowels for small and large, minor and major things respectively. It makes sense, too, as one vowel points down, one vowel points up. The three syllables on the right form the basis to many more Taisroan concepts.

To a certain degree, this sometimes lends itself to gender differentiation. Sometimes names add a i at the end to make it sound more feminine, the same goes for male names and a.

Sound Inversion is another important topic. Often times words that are related may sound similar. Taisroan has a theme of taking some opposites and inverting sounds between them. The table to the left shows some examples of this, as well as applications of sound sizing.

There's a few more varieties, and there is no solid consistant pattern, but this just happens to be something that... happens a lot in Taisroan, and may make words easier to memorize.

Syllabification

Syllables are a pretty cool guy, it involves the phonetic details when multiple syllables are put together. Syllabification in Taisroan is not a complex subject as it is very standardized.

There are always audible splits between syllables. Subtle, but present. You could say there is a slight glottal stop at the end of every syllable, or at least a quick reduction in voice. For instance, if the vowels a and í showed up next to each other, they may sound like the single vowel ý, but they would be on opposite sides of a syllable split, and would therefore be pronounced with a cadence that would prevent that confusion. The splits can be seen by dividing words into sections like those laid out above. Keep in mind that suffixes may cause consonant clusters with the final consonant of the root word, so it may be wise to analyze if the last syllable looks like a suffix to clarify its pronunciation.

mikalisvim is a word with a prefix and a suffix. Phonetically, it could be split up as “mi.ka.lis.vim”.

muémáfarzop is another example with both vowel and consonant clusters. With the same technique, it can be split into "mu-é.ma.far-zop". Again, a bit of knowledge of roots and fixes may be needed, but there aren't too many oddities.

Another quirky rule of syllabification is that if two of the same vowel are next to each other, they are pronounced as one vowel. The word muualak is merely pronounced "mu.a.lak".

Now, a big part of language pronounciation are stressed syllables. Taisroan handles syllable accents in an easy way. As in there is one rule and two exceptions. They are as follows.

  1. The accent is always placed upon the second syllable in a word.
    • Unless the second syllable is part of a prefix, which then the accent moves to the first non-prefix syllable.
    • Unless the word is a title of a person, in which it is placed on the first syllable.

Title of a person also counts toward names, though names may have variation in cadence.

Tonality is relatively simple, too. In single words, tone tends to rise as it approaches the stressed syllable and subtly fall after stressed syllables.

Section 3: Morphology

The morphology of Taisroan is not complex in the slightest. The language has a very "universal" quality to it, and so words aren't often altered very much.

Nouns & Pronouns

Nouns in Taisroan have no genders, no tenses, no cases, and no numbers. Pluralization is handled with a suffix that never changes (lo), tenses with prefixes (pẃ, me, bo).

In terms of Gender, Taisroan is almost entirely genderless, even in names for things. There are no words for male and female varieties of things (job titles, animals, kinship terms) and to specify gender, they just use a word or even just a suffix to specify. As one would expect, Pronouns are genderless as well. That's right, Taisroans get by in everyday life without words for "he" or "she". Taisroans use a singular "they" pronoun. This also functions as a passive pronoun (i.e. "One" in english). For hypotheticals and indirect statements. Taisroan also has a "self" pronoun which is used when referencing the subject as the object as well. "I talk to myself" would be "ha sẃ gẃl źetím."

So how does one specify gender when using Taisroa's completely-devoid-of-gender language? Taisroan has words for Male and Female that do not need their own table as they're not that important. They're þaþ, "þaþ" and þiþ, "þiþ" respectively.

Lets start off with particles.

The first particle, "gẃl", is a "transitive indicator". It's placed between the object and the verb to indicate an action is being performed on it. It mostly helps when differenciating between Subject-Verb sentences, and "Subject-Object-Verb" sentances with omitted/implied subjects. For example; "ha źetím" means "I speak". But "ha gẃl źetím." means "speak to me" or "tell me", and omits the object which would most likely be "ca", "you".

"tup" is a catch all word for "having" "bearing" "wearing" etc. It's inserted between an noun and a noun to use what proceeds it as an adjective, or adverb and a verb or adjective, to link the two. Example: "The silver haired child spoke." would be "sokip tup naśor di géqso źetím." It takes "Silver hair" and uses it as an adjective for something to describe the child. You could also think of "tup" as "with".

Copula have two varients in Taisroan. The first, þal, is used for noun = noun. Example: "I am an adult" would be "ha sokap þal."

The other, ðil, is used for noun/verb/anything = adjective/adverb. Example: "you are wise" would be "ca íga ðil."

Word Order

Taisroan is largely SOV, Subject-Object-Verb. The closest comparison I can make is to Japanese. Lets look at the phrase “this is a rock.”

Taisroan does not have quite as many particles, however. Intransitive verbs work similarly to the copula. Once you get to other transitive verbs, however, we have to use a "transitive indicator" to connect the object of the sentence to the verb being performed. Let's check out "I draw a Circle."

Noun Phrase Order

Noun phrases have a relatively simple structure. The subject comes first, or object, and whatever descriptors are attatched afterward.

we tup naśor di geqso gan kirino þal
we tup naśor di geqso gan kirino þal
the one with hair (that is) silver from kirino is

This would be Taisroan for "It's the silver haired man from kirino." Order of discriptors is somewhat flexible, but it is common practice to put more specific details closer to the subject. So you would list descriptors of something before you would say where they are from.

So "The guy from kirino with silver hair is speaking to the guy with the red eyes." Would be fully written out as

"we tup naśor di geqso gan kirino þal we tup qoki di tálẃ gẃl meźetím."
